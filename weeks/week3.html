Card Economics

Even as a PayTo-focused business, cards are your competitive context—what you're comparing costs against.

Merchant Service Fee = Interchange + Scheme Fees + Acquirer Margin
When a merchant accepts a card, this is what they pay. Understanding each component helps you articulate PayTo's cost advantage.
Interchange
Fee paid to the card-issuing bank. RBA regulates via benchmarks:
• Credit: 0.5% weighted average
• Debit: 8 cents weighted average
Varies by card type (premium cards = higher) and merchant category.
Scheme Fees
Paid to Visa/Mastercard/eftpos. Growing cost component, less transparent than interchange. RBA is examining these.
Least-Cost Routing (LCR)
Most Australian debit cards are dual-network (eftpos + Visa/Mastercard). LCR lets merchants route via cheaper network. eftpos often cheaper. RBA supports LCR as pro-competition.
FairPay Implication
If a merchant pays 1.5% on cards and you offer PayTo at 0.3%, that's compelling. But understand what you're comparing to, and build flexibility for regulatory changes (RBA conclusions coming end-March 2026).

Payments Technology Stack

As CEO, you govern architecture. Here are the layers and typical build/partner decisions:

Frontend / UXBuild (your differentiation)
Orchestration (routing, retries, webhooks)Build (core product logic)
Risk & Compliance (fraud, KYC)Buy tools + Build rules
Rail Connections (NPP, BECS, cards)Partner (sponsors/acquirers)
Ledger (balances, transactions)Build (source of truth)
ReconciliationBuild (ops efficiency)
Key Principle
Own orchestration (your product logic), partner for rail access (you're not becoming an NPP participant), buy commodity tools (fraud screening, KYC), build differentiated UX (merchant dashboard, agreement flows).
